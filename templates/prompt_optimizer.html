🎬 Lighting: The Soul of Your Image

Lighting is the most transformative element in AI image generation. The difference between a flat, amateurish image and a professional masterpiece often comes down to how you describe light. Never skip lighting descriptions. Specify the light source: "soft golden hour sunlight," "harsh studio lights," "neon cyberpunk glow," or "volumetric atmospheric fog lit from above." Include light quality: is it diffused and soft, or sharp and dramatic? Describe light direction: backlighting creates silhouettes and drama, side lighting emphasizes texture and dimension, front lighting reveals detail. Use temperature language: "warm amber light," "cool blue moonlight," "harsh white spotlight." The magic happens when you combine lighting with mood: "cinematic chiaroscuro lighting with deep shadows," "bioluminescent glowing undergrowth," "golden hour rim lighting on water." Professional Midjourney users spend 30-40% of their prompt on lighting specifications because it fundamentally transforms the image's emotional impact and technical quality. Add phrases like "dramatic lighting," "mood lighting," "professional photography lighting," or "studio lighting" to instantly elevate your results.

📷 Camera Angles & Composition

Camera perspective controls how your viewer experiences the image. An ultra-wide angle shot creates epic, overwhelming scale; a close-up macro shot reveals intimate detail; a Dutch angle creates unease and dynamic energy. Start by choosing your primary angle: bird's eye view, low angle looking up (cinematic and powerful), eye-level (natural and relatable), extreme close-up, or landscape wide shot. Add depth with compositional language: "depth of field," "shallow focus," "bokeh background," or "sharp foreground with blurred background." Use lens terminology to trigger specific visual effects: "35mm lens" feels intimate, "85mm lens" is cinematic and flattering, "14mm ultra-wide" creates dramatic perspective distortion, "macro photography" shows intricate detail. Frame your subject strategically using compositional rules: "rule of thirds," "golden ratio," "leading lines," "centered composition," "negative space." Camera movement language matters too: "tracking shot," "pan," "crane shot," "establishing shot," or "over-the-shoulder shot" all trigger specific framing patterns in Midjourney. The most successful prompts specify both what you want to see AND where in the frame it appears. Combine camera language with action: "low angle action shot," "overhead tactical view," "first-person perspective," each creating entirely different images from the same object.

🚫 Negative Prompts: Removing Visual Noise

Negative prompts are your secret weapon for controlling what Midjourney WON'T generate. They're equally important as positive descriptions. Start with quality control negatives: add "low quality, bad quality, blurry, distorted, deformed, ugly, amateur" to universally improve results. Include anatomy/technical negatives when needed: "bad hands, extra fingers, missing fingers, mutated limbs, anatomically incorrect" for human figures. Add specific rejection terms based on your vision: if you don't want text, add "text, watermark, letters, writing"; if you want realism, add "cartoon, anime, illustration, stylized"; if you want high quality, add "compression artifacts, pixelated, jpeg artifacts." Use medium-specific negatives: "3D render, CGI, video game" if you want photography or painting; "photorealistic" if you want stylized art. Avoid common mistakes: don't use negative prompts to say "not X" (use "exclude X" instead), and don't list contradictory terms. The best approach is adding 5-8 specific negative prompt terms that protect your creative vision. Every prompt should include "--no low quality, blurry" as a baseline. Advanced users craft negative prompts as carefully as positive ones, understanding that sometimes what you exclude matters more than what you include.
